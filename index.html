About Tokimura

1928: Preserved Fragment
for Your Breath.

Built in 1928, this house preserves the traditional Japanese way of life, its spaces gently divided only by fusuma doors and shoji screens. This is not a place for socializing, but a preserved fragment of time to indulge in the luxury of solitude.

The soft light filtering through the shoji, the subtle scent of tatami mats, and the overwhelming silence of the night. Here, taking off your watch and simply listening to the sound of your own breath will become the most precious experience of your modern journey.

We named this inn (ryokan) "Tokimura"—literally translating to the "Village of Time." Our wish is for this house to serve two purposes: first, to be your anchor for traveling through the immense layers of time accumulated within the town of Mikuni, Fukui. And second, to provide a space where you can listen to the sound of your own breath and the emotions that well up moment by moment, inviting you on a deep, introspective journey into the present.

The Depths of History and Culture

Wealth Transformed
into Spirit.

As a prominent port of call for the Kitamaebune—a massive fleet of legendary merchant ships with thousands of vessels in operation at its peak—Mikuni accumulated immense wealth. During the Edo and early Meiji eras, it was this northern sea route, rather than the Pacific, that served as the primary stage for Japan’s logistics and cultural dissemination.

Instead of pursuing personal luxury, Mikuni's prosperous merchants invested their fortunes in building schools "Ryushokan", developing the harbor "Esser Dike", and inviting cultural figures to cultivate a richness of spirit.

The elegance that still permeates the streets today, and the breathing tradition of places like the over 150-year-old "Uoshiro" restaurant, are the lingering fragrances of their noble aesthetic.

A Remaining Miracle

Saved by
the Sidelines.

During the Meiji era, when Japan's railway network was being established, Mikuni was left out of the main logistics routes. Having escaped rapid modernization and the fires of war, its dignified streets and unhurried flow of time remain untouched today.

13 Million Years Ago: Nature’s Cathedral

The story begins with the earth’s ancient memory. The rugged cliffs of Tojinbo and the sacred, uninhabited island of Oshima were forged by volcanic fury 13 million years ago. These majestic columnar formations rise like a magnificent cathedral of stone, where the eternal forces of nature speak in deep, echoing tones.

1375: Medieval Stillness

As you step into the town, time shifts to the Middle Ages. At Takidanji Temple, the oldest in Mikuni, moss has quietly accumulated for over 650 years, absorbing the light and the silence. Every step along its ancient stone path invites you to dissolve into medieval tranquility, far removed from the present world.

1541: The Unquenchable Passion

Behind the silence, a fire of devotion burns. Since 1541, Mikuni Shrine has stood as the guardian of the town’s spirit. This prayer culminates in the Mikuni Festival, a 300-year-old tradition where giant warrior floats—reaching 6.5 meters in height—shake the historic streets. It is the lifeblood of Mikuni, pulsing with a passion that never fades.

1873: Echoes of Elegance

With the dawn of the modern era, Mikuni flourished as a cultural gateway. Uoshiro, founded in 1873, was once a renowned stage for social and cultural exchange in the historic district. Today, within its walls that still house the memories of that era, a Kyoto-trained master prepares local delicacies, blending the town’s rich history with its vibrant present.

1882: The Horizon’s Curve

In 1882, a Dutch engineer named Esser reshaped the coast with a visionary brick breakwater. The Esser Dike remains a monument to Meiji-era ingenuity, its beautiful curve protecting the harbor for over a century. When its silhouette is painted in the vivid colors of the sunset, you feel the quiet harmony between human engineering and the vast Sea of Japan.

Access

From Kyoto

(Approx. 2.5 hours)

Take the Limited Express "Thunderbird" to JR Fukui Station. Transfer to the Echizen Railway (Mikuni-Awara Line) and disembark at Mikuni Station. *Note: Be careful not to confuse this with the final stop, "Mikuni-Minato Station." The house is a 10-minute walk away. I highly recommend a route through the atmospheric old streets of the Kitamaebune era.

From Kanazawa

(Approx. 1h 30m)

Take the JR local train or Shinkansen to JR Awaraonsen Station. Board the Keifuku Bus (Route 85 or 97) and alight at Eishoji (Tatemachi), a stop in the heart of the historic district. This cost-effective and scenic route offers a slower transition from the city’s pace to our quiet harbor.

From Takayama

(Approx. 3.5 – 4 hours)

Take an express bus (Nohi Bus) from Takayama to Kanazawa Station. From Kanazawa, follow the coastal route via JR Awaraonsen Station as described above. This "Mountains to Sea" route connects Japan’s mountainous heart to its historic coastal gateway.
